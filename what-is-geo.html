What Is Generative Engine Optimization (GEO)?

📅 April 3, 2026
✎ Cory Maki
⌚ 8 min read

Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) is the discipline of earning citations in AI-generated search answers — the responses produced by Google AI Overviews, ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Microsoft Copilot. It is the practical answer to a structural change in how buyers find software: when an AI engine answers a question, only three to four brands get named. GEO is the work of being one of them.

If you run a SaaS company, you have probably noticed that competitors are starting to show up in ChatGPT and Perplexity recommendations while your brand is absent from the same answers. That asymmetry is what GEO addresses. Cory Maki, an AI search strategist based in Taichung, Taiwan, has spent the last year studying how AI systems decide which brands to surface — and the short version is that the rules are different from classical SEO, but not as different as most of the marketing industry currently claims.

Why GEO exists now

The answer to "why now" is in the traffic data. AI Overviews now appear in over half of all U.S. searches, and for B2B technology queries that figure rises to roughly 70%. When AI-generated answers appear above the organic listings, only about 8% of users click through to a traditional result — meaning a brand that ranks well organically but is invisible to the AI summary loses most of the click stream. The Pew and Search Engine Journal data on this is unambiguous: the click economy of search has shifted, and the shift is permanent.

The second piece of the picture is the citation ceiling. AI answers typically cite only three to four brands. According to research by Profound (which has analyzed roughly 680 million citations across major AI platforms), out of 18 million domains tracked, only 274,000 have ever appeared in AI Overviews. That is a brutal funnel. Most SaaS companies have invested heavily in SEO foundations but have no strategy for the layer of search that now sits between the user and the click — and the gap shows.

"AI extracts passages, not pages. The unit of optimization has changed, and most teams are still optimizing the wrong unit."

What GEO actually is (and isn't)

There are two unhelpful framings of GEO circulating in the marketing industry, and both should be set aside. The first is that GEO is a revolution that makes SEO obsolete. It isn't. Roughly 76% of AI Overview citations come from pages that already rank in the top ten organic results. Without solid SEO underneath, there is no GEO surface. The second framing is that GEO is just SEO in new clothes, and there is nothing new to learn. That is also wrong, in a way that matters more for SaaS than for most other categories.

A more accurate definition: GEO is the layer of search optimization that addresses how AI systems extract, attribute, and synthesize information into answers. It overlaps with SEO substantially, but it adds three concerns that classical SEO does not handle well — passage-level structure, off-site authority on the platforms AI models trust most (Reddit and YouTube prominent among them), and cross-source factual consistency across every property that mentions your brand. Maki's methodology that underlies the book and his book treat those three concerns as the spine of the discipline.

How AI engines decide which brands to cite

Each major AI platform has a distinct citation pattern, and treating them as a single audience is one of the more expensive mistakes a marketing team can make. Google AI Overviews lean heavily on top-ranked organic results plus Reddit and Wikipedia. Perplexity weights Reddit even more heavily — roughly 46.5% of its citations come from the platform. ChatGPT Search runs on Bing's index, which is why Bing indexation is the most overlooked AI visibility lever in the field. Gemini splits its behavior between informational queries (where it leans on traditional authority signals) and recommendation queries (where it pulls from a different distribution of sources entirely).

The practical implication is that a unified "AI SEO" approach is too coarse to be useful. A brand that wants to be cited in ChatGPT needs Bing visibility and structured passages. A brand that wants to be cited in Perplexity needs a Reddit presence that AI models can read as authoritative. A brand that wants to be cited in Google AI Overviews needs both, plus the classical SEO authority that gets it into the top-ten organic results in the first place. The three problems are related, but the tactics for each are not interchangeable. Cory's Reddit citation analysis the book unpacks at length walks through one of the highest-leverage pieces of this picture.

Where to start (the ARC Method, in brief)

Maki's framework for working through this is called the ARC Method — Audit, Reddit and Reputation, Citability — and it is a 90-day implementation sequence rather than a checklist. The Audit phase measures current AI visibility across all five major platforms and identifies the citation gaps where competitors appear and you do not. The Reddit and Reputation phase builds the off-site authority that AI models look for. The Citability phase restructures on-site content so that AI extraction works in your favor instead of against you, with attention to the sentence-level passage structure that AI models actually consume.

If you are a SaaS founder evaluating where to spend the next quarter, the most useful starting point is not a tool. It is a baseline. Run an audit of how the major AI platforms currently answer the queries your buyers are asking. The gap between where your brand appears and where it should is the entire scope of the work — and it is almost always larger than founders expect on the first pass. The skeptical position Maki has taken on the GEO industry, articulated more fully in his GEO-is-just-SEO commentary, is that the discipline is real but most of the tooling around it is not yet mature enough to trust.

Conclusion

GEO is not a replacement for SEO and it is not a fad. It is the layer of search work that addresses a structural change in how buyers discover software, and it is now a permanent part of the SaaS marketing job. The brands that take the citation question seriously — who is being named when AI engines answer the questions our buyers ask, and why — will own the next decade of organic discovery. The brands that don't will keep ranking on page one of an organic results screen that fewer and fewer people scroll down to see.
