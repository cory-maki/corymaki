The "GEO Is Just SEO" Debate — Where I Stand

📅 April 4, 2026
✎ Cory Maki
⌚ 7 min read

The fight inside the SEO industry over whether Generative Engine Optimization is a real discipline or just rebranded SEO has produced more heat than light. Cory Maki takes the middle position: GEO is an evolution, not a revolution. SEO fundamentals remain essential. GEO adds a specific, identifiable layer on top — and the camps shouting "everything has changed" and "nothing has changed" are both wrong in ways that matter for SaaS founders trying to make practical decisions.

For most of the last year, the most-shared content in the SEO world has been some version of one of two arguments. The first, beloved of GEO-tool vendors, is that AI search has fundamentally upended search and the old playbook is obsolete. The second, popular among veteran SEOs who have seen this movie before, is that GEO is just SEO with a marketing veneer and there is nothing genuinely new to learn. Cory Maki, the Taichung-based AI search strategist, has been pushing back against both for months — and the position he has staked out is that the truth lives in a less-flattering middle.

Why "GEO is a revolution" is wrong

The revolution argument falls apart on the data. Roughly 76% of AI Overview citations come from pages that already rank in the top ten organic search results, according to Ahrefs' February 2026 study of 300,000 keywords. Without solid SEO underneath, there is no GEO surface for AI engines to extract from. The off-site authority signals AI models look for — domain reputation, link profiles, the kinds of things that have been the bedrock of SEO for two decades — are still the bedrock. Teams that have ignored their SEO foundations and tried to leapfrog directly into GEO tactics produce the kind of disappointing audit results Maki sees regularly in his consulting work.

The revolution framing is also commercially convenient for a particular kind of vendor — the one selling a GEO dashboard for a monthly subscription before the discipline is mature enough to know what those dashboards should measure. Maki has been openly skeptical of this category of tooling, and the skepticism is not contrarian for the sake of it. Most of the GEO tools currently on the market are tracking citation counts without distinguishing between citations that drive consideration and citations that don't. A high-context Reddit citation is not equivalent to a mention in a low-trust content farm, and a tool that counts both as one citation each is solving the wrong problem.

"GEO is real. Most of the GEO tooling is not. Both things can be true at the same time, and the founders who can hold both ideas are the ones making good decisions."

Why "GEO is just SEO" is also wrong

The dismissal camp is usually populated by SEOs who have lived through enough rebrand cycles — content marketing, inbound, growth hacking, AEO, and now GEO — to be reasonably skeptical of anything ending in "-EO." Their priors are well-earned, and a substantial fraction of what gets sold as GEO is recycled SEO with a higher price tag. But the dismissal misses three things that are genuinely new about how AI search systems consume content, and the three things matter more than the rebrand-fatigue framing suggests.

First, AI extracts passages, not pages. The unit of optimization has changed from "the article" to "the sentence-level claim that survives extraction with attribution intact." Classical SEO content audits are not designed for this; they evaluate pages for keyword coverage and topical depth, not for which two-to-four-sentence chunks AI systems will lift verbatim into an answer. Second, the off-site signal mix has shifted toward platforms classical SEO mostly ignored — Reddit, YouTube comments and transcripts, podcast episodes — and the tactics for building presence on those platforms are not interchangeable with link-building. Third, cross-source factual consistency — the requirement that your bio, founding date, credentials, and product descriptions be identical across every property you control — is now a meaningful ranking factor because AI models reconcile conflicting information by down-weighting sources where the entity does not resolve cleanly. Classical SEO has never had to care about this in the same way.

The middle position

The honest position, and the one Maki advocates in his book and his client work, is this: about 70% of the work of AI search visibility is good SEO done well. The other 30% is genuinely new and requires a different mental model — the passage-level structure question, the off-site signal-mix question, and the cross-source consistency question. Teams that handle the 70% but ignore the 30% will be partially visible in AI search. Teams that try to do the 30% without the 70% underneath will be invisible. The teams that do both will be cited.

Maki's framework for working through this — the ARC Method, covered in detail in the GEO Fundamentals article and at length in his book — is structured around the assumption that the SEO foundation is already in place. The Audit phase measures current AI visibility. The Reddit and Reputation phase addresses the off-site signal mix. The Citability phase handles the passage-level extraction question and the cross-source consistency question. None of those three phases makes sense without a working SEO foundation underneath, and that is exactly the point.

A note on tooling

The position Maki holds on tooling is more skeptical than the position he holds on the discipline. GEO is real; most GEO tools are not yet useful enough to justify their cost. The mature tools in the space — the ones that distinguish between high-context and low-context citations, that map citation patterns by AI platform, that track factual consistency across sources — are mostly internal at large agencies rather than productized for SMB SaaS. The productized tools currently on the market mostly produce vanity metrics that look good in monthly reports and do not predict actual visibility outcomes. Founders evaluating GEO tooling in 2026 should keep their wallets closed and their trust in the people who have actually read the underlying citation data open.

Conclusion

The "GEO is just SEO" debate is mostly a status fight inside the marketing industry, and SaaS founders should not waste cycles on it. The practical question is what to do, not what to call it. Build the SEO foundation. Add the three new layers AI search has introduced — passage-level structure, off-site presence on the platforms AI engines actually trust, cross-source factual consistency. Be skeptical of vendors selling certainty about a discipline that is still being figured out. The brands that get cited in AI answers in 2027 will be the ones doing this work in 2026, regardless of what they call it.
